## 1. Academy overview

Scholar Basketball Academy (SBA) is Singapore's first and largest mall-based basketball academy. Founded in 2014 under founder and camp director Wong Wei Long, the academy has trained more than 1,000 young athletes — from complete beginners to national youth players — over the past decade. Its main office and indoor court sit inside Parkway Parade at 80 Marine Parade Rd, #07-16/17, Singapore 449269, on the mall's Level 7 enrichment hub. From this base, SBA operates six islandwide training locations: the Parkway Parade home court plus five satellite venues at Singapore Chinese Girls School, Anglo-Chinese School (Barker Road), North London Collegiate School Singapore, Canadian International School of Singapore, and a sixth venue *verify with Kevin*.

The academy offers a progression-style program portfolio. **SBA Kids** introduces ages 3–7 to the sport through fun, beginner-friendly motor-skill and coordination work; **SBA Development** carries ages 5–18 through structured fundamentals (ball handling, shooting, footwork); and **SBA Competitive** takes advanced players (roughly ages 12–16) into high-intensity skill and team strategy work plus 16+ youth league games annually in the SGPBBL. Sitting alongside the regular programs are **SBA Coaches** workshops (3-hour sessions for basketball coaches and educators), **Private 1-on-1 training**, a complimentary **1.5-hour free trial**, MOE-aligned **holiday camps** for boys and girls ages 5–14, and the **DSA Elite Program** — a progressive 4-day intensive for players preparing for Direct School Admission basketball trials. Camps maintain a 1:10 camper-to-trainer ratio and are designed by professional trainers under Wong Wei Long's direction. The team currently stands at 15+ people across the organisation, with around 15+ coaches on staff (9 are currently profiled on the website).

On the platform side, SBA runs scholarbasketball.com on WordPress + Elementor, with Mindbody as the CRM and class-booking layer, Amelia as the camp-booking engine (payments processed in SGD via Stripe), and Jotform for waivers and indemnity forms. As a business, SBA is bootstrapped and profit-based, with $200K+ ARR and no external runway dependency — growth is self-funded and earned, not financed.

## 2. Target audience

The primary buyer is a Singapore parent, roughly aged 30–50, enrolling a child aged 3–18 in basketball training. The end user, however, is the child — which creates a dual-persona dynamic: the parent signs the cheque and renews the membership, but the kid's experience on the court is what drives whether that renewal actually happens. Good marketing has to land both sides at once. Geographically, the demand concentrates around two corridors: families in eastern Singapore who anchor on the Marine Parade catchment around the Parkway Parade home court, and families in the Bukit Timah / Dunearn area who use the SCGS and ACS Barker satellite venues. The remaining islandwide footprint (NLCS, CIS, and the sixth venue *verify with Kevin*) extends reach beyond those two cores.

A meaningful secondary segment is parents pursuing the DSA (Direct School Admission) basketball pathway, looking to give their child a credible route into a top secondary school. They behave differently from general-skills parents: longer planning horizon, higher tolerance for intensity, more sensitive to track record. On the other side, two segments SBA is *not* trying to serve are kids who only want casual pickup play with friends, and parents shopping purely on price for the cheapest court-time coaching available — both will churn out of a structured progression model.

## 4. Player / kid persona

The end user of SBA is the child, aged 3–18 — not the buyer. That distinction matters because the kid is the one whose experience determines whether the parent renews. Kids care about fun drills, getting to play real games (5-on-5 scrimmages, SGPBBL matches), feeling skilled, having friends in the program, and getting to use new gear. What makes a kid want to come back week after week is the feeling of visible progress — a skill jump they can show off — combined with coach attention (the 1:10 camp ratio matters here), being on a winning team, and parent praise after sessions.

What makes kids quit is the opposite set of forces: boredom from too much drilling and not enough scrimmage, feeling singled out in a negative way, having no friends in their training group, and schedule fatigue from stacking basketball on top of school and tuition. This is why a parent-facing churn signal — a kid's attendance dropping in Mindbody — usually shows up *before* the parent overtly cancels. Diagnosing it properly means looking at the kid's experience on the court, not just the parent's stated reason. The marketing implication: content and renewal nudges should reach both audiences — proof points and outcomes for the parent, and visible-progress moments (highlights, scrimmage clips, coach shoutouts) that the parent can show the kid.

## 5. Problems & pain points

**Core problem (parent-side):**

Singapore parents want their child to develop physically, socially, and emotionally through sport — but the basketball options available to them are fragmented and hard to evaluate. School CCAs vary in quality from one school to the next and rarely focus on individual progression. Pickup-court coaching from unverified "uncle coaches" is cheap but inconsistent, with no curriculum, no insurance, and no pathway. One-off holiday camps from sports-event companies fill quickly and don't build a coaching relationship. For families with DSA ambitions, the Direct School Admission window adds a high-stakes deadline that makes the wrong choice expensive — a wasted year of training can mean a different secondary school placement. Founder Wong Wei Long's brand provides the credibility shortcut these families need in a market full of unverified coaches: a recognisable national-level player vouching for the curriculum and personally vetting the roster.

**Specific pain points heard from parents (verbatim where possible from `/articles` blog, FB reviews, IG comments — pull on next refresh):**

- "Is my kid behind?" — comparison anxiety vs other parents' kids
- "Will DSA be too competitive?" — DSA is high-stakes, limited slots
- "Outdoor courts in Singapore are too hot / unsafe (haze, rain)" — drives demand for aircon indoor
- "I don't know if this coach is actually qualified" — distrust of pickup coaches
- "We tried [Academy X], the coach changed every term" — coach continuity matters
- "The drive across the island is killing us" — convenience by location matters
- "My kid lost interest after a few weeks" — engagement / fun balance

**What it costs them:**
- DSA-track families: missed window means a different secondary school placement
- Kids-track families: kid drops basketball entirely, switches to less active hobby
- Time: parent time driving across Singapore, signing forms, juggling schedules

**Emotional tension:**
- Fear of choosing the "wrong" academy (wasted year + wasted money)
- Pressure to keep up with peer parents' choices
- Guilt over signing a kid up for something they end up not liking

## 7. Differentiation

**Key differentiators:**

1. **Mall-based aircon court** — Singapore's first; weather-proof, parent-comfortable, sibling-friendly waiting area.
2. **Founder brand (Wong Wei Long)** — Singapore's most visible basketball player, lending credibility no other academy can replicate.
3. **Scale + tenure** — 1,000+ athletes since 2014, 15+ coaches, 6 locations.
4. **Structured progression pathway** — Kids (3–7) → Development (5–18) → Competitive → DSA Elite / National-youth.
5. **Camper-to-trainer ratio 1:10** (camps) — personalised attention.
6. **DSA Elite Program** — dedicated 4-day intensive for DSA basketball trials, with placement track record.
7. **SGPBBL competitive play** — 16+ youth league games annually (Competitive program), real game reps.

**How we do it differently:**

Most Singapore basketball options are either coach-personality-led — one star coach whose availability and mood gate the whole experience — or facility-led — a court rental plus a freelance coach booked by the hour, with no curriculum binding the two together. SBA is structured differently: it runs as a vertically integrated academy, owning the curriculum, the coach roster, the venue partnerships (Parkway Parade plus five school venues), and the booking systems (Mindbody for classes, Amelia for camps, Jotform for waivers). That integration is why a kid can start at age 3 in SBA Kids and stay through to DSA Elite at 13, with the same brand, the same coaching standards, and the same progression logic carrying them across nearly a decade of development.

**Why customers choose us:**
- Trust shortcut via Wong Wei Long brand
- Geographic convenience (6 locations)
- Aircon comfort (no other academy has this)
- Tangible career-relevant outcome: DSA placements + SGPBBL games

## 11. Brand voice

**Tone:** confident, energetic, sporty. Not corporate. Not childish. Premium-athletic.

**Audience-direction:** Parent-facing, not kid-facing. Even copy about kid programs is written *to the parent about the kid*, not to the kid.

**Brand values:** Creativity · Dedication · Growth · Determination.

**Headline patterns:**
- Italic-emphasis on ONE word per headline (replicating `<em class="font-['LTEnergy-italic']">` pattern from site)
  - Example: "Build their *game*, their way."
  - Example: "Train *like* a national-team player."
- Short, punchy verbs as CTAs: "Book", "Train", "Play", "Sign up"
- Avoid long compound sentences in headlines

**Body voice:**
- Direct, conversational, second-person ("your child", "you'll see")
- Specific over generic — say "1:10 ratio" not "small group sizes"
- Lead with the parent's concern, then the SBA answer
- Light credentialing — drop one proof point per paragraph, not every sentence

**Personality (3–5 adjectives):**
- Confident
- Structured
- Local (Singapore-rooted)
- Family-aware
- Achievement-oriented

## 15. Renewal & retention drivers

**Why families re-sign each term:**
- Kid wants to keep going (the #1 driver — kid engagement = parent renewal)
- Visible skill progress (parent sees improvement at home / in games)
- Schedule continuity (kid's slot is already in the family calendar)
- Friendships built within the program
- Coach continuity (same coach each term — strong predictor of renewal)
- Proximity to DSA / SGPBBL season (parents won't pause mid-prep)

**Why families churn:**
- Kid loses interest (boredom, too much drilling, no friends, schedule fatigue)
- Schedule clash (tuition load increases, especially P5/P6 academic pressure)
- Family moves house / changes school
- Cheaper / closer alternative appears
- Negative experience with a specific coach
- Kid plateaus and parent thinks they need a different methodology
- DSA cycle ends (kid placed — parent disengages)

**Detection signals (from Mindbody):**
- Attendance drop-off > 2 consecutive sessions
- No re-enrolment for the next term within X days of term end
- Drop from regular program but signed up for camp only (downgrade signal)

**Retention plays (current + opportunities):**
- Term-end parent communication (current — informal via coach WhatsApp)
- Progress reports per term (opportunity — not yet systematised)
- Parent-of-the-month / kid-of-the-month features (community)
- Referral incentives (opportunity)
- Cross-sell from regular program to camps (current — informal)
- Cross-sell from kids to development to competitive (natural progression)
